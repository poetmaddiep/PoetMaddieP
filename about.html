About Maddie P.

My Journey

I’ve always wanted to be a poet. My earliest memory of writing poetry was in first grade for a Mother’s Day assignment. I remember rhyming "mommy" with "bunny" and "sunny." As you can tell, I wasn’t very good. But I stuck with it.

At 9 years old, I started attending annual summer writing workshops. My favorite part—next to collaborating with kids my age—was reading my work aloud and listening to others do the same. Writing became my escape during middle school as I struggled with social anxiety and depression. I shared my work with teachers who praised me, but I never believed my words were special.

Eventually, I began performing. At 14, I competed in my first poetry slam. Though I didn’t win, the experience made me obsessed with the rush of performing. By 16, I earned a spot on the Meta-4-Houston youth poetry team, later becoming Houston’s Youth Poet Laureate. Even with imposter syndrome and stage fright, I found a deep connection to the art of spoken word.

What Drives Me

I honestly think I might be addicted to performing. I love the energy of fellow poets performing and socializing. And how quickly these spaces feel like home and the people, family. Performing improves my mood. I value creativity, connection, and growth, and attending poetry slams and open mics provides me space to practice these values.

Occasionally, people tell me they feel seen by my words. Given the seriousness of my poems, I’m happy to know I can put into words these thoughts and feelings that someone else is also experiencing. Mental illness and racism can be such lonely experiences, so to bring a sense of "I see you" to people is worth it.

Poetry encompasses so much of my life that I can't ever imagine stopping. My poems are extremely personal to me, as many deal with what it’s like to be Black in America and navigate the complexities of mental health.

Accomplishments

My poem "To Be a Black Girl" was published in the New York Times. I was Houston’s Youth Poet Laureate, and I was invited by Voters of Tomorrow to perform at Austin’s Capitol for the March on Education, a protest against banned books. I also spent three years on the youth poetry team Meta-4-Houston.

Moreover, I frequently partner with Leaders Igniting Generational Healing and Transformation (LIGHT) as a featured performer and workshop host, where I delve into the connection between mental health and poetry. I also serve as a poetry judge for various events.

Looking Forward

Ideally, I would like to partner with organizations and people who promote education, justice, and equity, and mental health support in Black and Brown communities. Of course, I’d also like to publish a poetry book about navigating my mental illness in my 20s.

I hope to feature at more events across the country and forge new literary and general community connections. I'm not hoping to change the world with my poetry, but if I can make a few people feel heard and a little less lonely, that's perfect for me. Poetry has given me so much, and my goal is to keep giving back.
