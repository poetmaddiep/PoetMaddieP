About Maddie P.

My Journey

Hi, I’m Maddie, a spoken word artist whose poetry is deeply rooted in themes of mental health, trauma, and resilience. My journey started when I found solace in words during my darkest moments, and poetry became my lifeline. Over the years, I’ve performed at various events, collaborated with inspiring organizations, and discovered the power of vulnerability through art.

What Drives Me

There’s something electric about performing poetry—it’s addictive. The energy in the room, the connection between poets, and the way these spaces feel like home make it impossible for me to walk away. Poetry gives me a space to express myself in ways I’ve never been able to otherwise. Beyond that, it allows me to connect with others in a way that feels meaningful. When someone tells me they feel seen by my words, I know the risk of vulnerability is worth it. Mental illness and racism are isolating experiences, and if I can make someone feel less alone, that’s everything to me. For me, poetry is more than performance—it’s about creativity, connection, and growth. Writing about being Black in America, mental health struggles, and everything in between is a way of not only processing my world but inviting others to process theirs, too. Poetry isn’t just what I do; it’s who I am.

Accomplishments

Over the years, I’ve been fortunate to perform for organizations such as XYZ Mental Health Foundation and the National Poetry Alliance. I’ve also had the privilege of performing at venues like The Grand Slam Poetry Festival and collaborating with fellow artists to raise awareness about mental health.

  • Featured Poet: The Grand Slam Poetry Festival
  • Collaboration with XYZ Mental Health Foundation
  • Tedx Talk: "Poetry, Healing, and Breaking Stigmas"

Looking Forward

Looking ahead, I hope to continue using poetry as a tool for education, justice, and connection. I’d love to partner with organizations that prioritize equity and mental health support in Black and Brown communities, bringing poetry workshops to recreation centers and schools in underserved neighborhoods. Publishing a book about navigating mental illness in my twenties is also on the horizon. More than anything, I want to continue creating spaces where people feel heard and understood. If my words can help even a few people feel less lonely, that’s enough for me. Poetry has given me so much, and my goal is to keep giving back—to the art form, to the community, and to everyone searching for connection in the power of a well-chosen word./p>
